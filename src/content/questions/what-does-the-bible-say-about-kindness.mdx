# What does the Bible say about kindness?

## Introduction

The Bible speaks extensively about kindness, highlighting it as a fundamental characteristic of a believer's life. Kindness is not merely a social nicety; it is an essential virtue that reflects God's nature and His desires for humanity. Understanding what the Bible says about kindness can guide Christians in their relationships with others and encourage them to embody this trait in their daily lives. Explore this topic in depth at [Biible.net](https://biible.net?q=What%20does%20the%20Bible%20say%20about%20kindness?). 

In a world that often emphasizes self-interest and competition, kindness serves as a countercultural force. It fosters community, builds relationships, and reflects the love of Christ. The scriptures provide a wealth of guidance on how kindness operates both in interpersonal relationships and within the broader community of faith. By exploring the biblical foundation for kindness, readers can discover its importance and learn how to cultivate it actively.

This article will delve into key biblical passages that discuss kindness, offering insights into their context, teachings, and practical applications. By examining these scriptures, we aim to paint a comprehensive picture of what kindness means in the Christian faith and how it can be effectively practiced in our lives today.

## Key Bible Passages

### Ephesians 4:32

- **Context**: The Apostle Paul writes to the church in Ephesus, encouraging them to embrace their new identity in Christ. This letter offers practical guidance for living out the Christian faith in community.
- **What it teaches**: Ephesians 4:32 states, “Be kind and compassionate to one another, forgiving each other, just as in Christ God forgave you.” This passage emphasizes the importance of kindness as an expression of forgiveness and compassion.
- **Relevance**: This verse is relevant as it links kindness directly to the Christian experience of forgiveness. It calls believers to reflect the merciful character of God in their interactions with others.
- **Application**: Practically, believers can apply this teaching by consciously choosing to forgive and show compassion, especially in difficult relationships, thereby fostering a more loving community.

### Colossians 3:12

- **Context**: Paul addresses the Colossian church, instructing them on how to live out their faith in practical terms. The letter emphasizes moral conduct that stems from a transformed life in Christ.
- **What it teaches**: Colossians 3:12 declares, “Therefore, as God’s chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness, and patience.” This verse frames kindness as a necessary garment for believers.
- **Relevance**: The instruction to "clothe yourselves" indicates that kindness should be a deliberate choice in the Christian life, not merely an afterthought.
- **Application**: In everyday life, individuals can practice kindness by being intentional in their words and actions, treating others with the dignity and respect that they deserve.

### Proverbs 11:17

- **Context**: The book of Proverbs is a collection of wise sayings attributed to Solomon, aimed at imparting wisdom for ethical living and decision-making.
- **What it teaches**: Proverbs 11:17 states, “Those who are kind benefit themselves, but the cruel bring ruin on themselves.” This verse highlights the reciprocal nature of kindness.
- **Relevance**: This proverb underscores that kindness is not only beneficial to others but also brings personal rewards, suggesting a moral economy where good deeds return to the doer.
- **Application**: Believers can take heart in this promise and be encouraged to practice kindness, knowing that their acts will bear fruit in their own lives as well.

### Galatians 5:22-23

- **Context**: Paul writes to the churches in Galatia to clarify the implications of faith and the role of the Holy Spirit in a believer’s life.
- **What it teaches**: In Galatians 5:22-23, kindness is listed as one of the fruits of the Spirit, indicating that it naturally emerges when one is living in accordance with the Holy Spirit.
- **Relevance**: This connection illustrates that kindness is not simply a moral obligation but a byproduct of a life transformed by the Holy Spirit.
- **Application**: Christians are encouraged to cultivate their relationship with the Holy Spirit, allowing kindness to flow naturally from their lives as they grow in faith.

### Luke 6:35

- **Context**: Jesus, during the Sermon on the Plain, teaches a radical approach to love and ethics, challenging common societal norms.
- **What it teaches**: Luke 6:35 says, “But love your enemies, do good to them, and lend to them without expecting to get anything back.” This passage expands the definition of kindness to include not just friends but even adversaries.
- **Relevance**: This radical call to kindness emphasizes that true kindness goes beyond convenience and comfort, reflecting the unconditional love of God.
- **Application**: Practicing this kind of kindness may involve acts of love towards those who may not reciprocate, fostering a spirit of grace and mercy in challenging relationships.
