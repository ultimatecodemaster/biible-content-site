# What does the Bible say about discipleship?

## Introduction

Discipleship is a central theme in the Bible, highlighting the relationship between Jesus and His followers. At its core, discipleship involves not only believing in Christ but also actively following Him, learning from His teachings, and living out those principles in daily life. Understanding what the Bible says about discipleship is essential for anyone seeking to grow in their faith and deepen their relationship with God.

The importance of discipleship cannot be overstated; it shapes the identity of believers and the mission of the church. As we delve into the Scriptures, we will explore the biblical foundation of discipleship, its implications for personal growth, and its call to engage with others in faith. By examining key passages, we will uncover the essence of what it means to be a disciple of Christ. ## Key Bible Passages

### Matthew 28:19-20

- **Context**: Known as the Great Commission, this passage is spoken by Jesus to His disciples after His resurrection. It encapsulates the mission of the early church and serves as a directive for all believers.
- **What it teaches**: Jesus commands His followers to make disciples of all nations, baptizing them and teaching them to obey His commands. This passage highlights the comprehensive nature of discipleship, which includes evangelism, teaching, and community.
- **Relevance**: It underscores the global mission of the church and the responsibility of every believer to participate in discipleship. This command is foundational for understanding the purpose of the church.
- **Application**: Today, this passage calls Christians to actively share their faith with others, engage in teaching and mentoring, and support one another in their spiritual journeys.

### Luke 9:23

- **Context**: In this verse, Jesus addresses a crowd, outlining the essential requirements for being His disciple during His ministry. It serves as a reminder of the sacrifices involved in following Him.
- **What it teaches**: Jesus emphasizes self-denial, taking up one’s cross daily, and following Him. This indicates that true discipleship requires commitment, resilience, and a willingness to prioritize Jesus above all else.
- **Relevance**: This teaching confronts contemporary notions of comfort and convenience in faith, reminding believers that discipleship often comes with challenges and sacrifices.
- **Application**: Christians are encouraged to evaluate their priorities and make intentional choices that align with their commitment to Christ, even if it requires personal sacrifice.

### John 8:31-32

- **Context**: Jesus speaks to Jews who had believed in Him, clarifying the nature of true discipleship. His words come amidst His teachings about spiritual truth and freedom.
- **What it teaches**: This passage establishes that genuine discipleship involves abiding in Jesus' word, which leads to knowing the truth and experiencing freedom from sin.
- **Relevance**: It highlights the transformative power of Scripture and the importance of a personal relationship with Jesus as central to discipleship.
- **Application**: Believers are encouraged to immerse themselves in Scripture, allowing it to shape their thoughts and actions, thus fostering a deeper understanding of truth.

### Acts 2:42

- **Context**: Following Pentecost, this verse describes the early church's devotion to the apostles' teachings, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayer, illustrating the communal aspect of discipleship.
- **What it teaches**: The early followers of Christ committed themselves to learning, community, and worship, which are vital components of a disciple's life.
- **Relevance**: This passage serves as a blueprint for contemporary church life, emphasizing the importance of community in the discipleship process.
- **Application**: Modern believers are called to engage actively in their church communities, participating in teaching, fellowship, and communal worship.

### 2 Timothy 2:2

- **Context**: Paul writes to Timothy, emphasizing the importance of passing on the teachings of Christ to reliable individuals who can teach others, reflecting a generational aspect of discipleship.
- **What it teaches**: This passage illustrates that discipleship is not only about personal growth but also involves mentoring others and sharing one’s faith.
- **Relevance**: It highlights the role of teaching and mentorship in the Christian faith, establishing a chain of discipleship that extends beyond individual believers.
- **Application**: Christians are encouraged to invest in others by teaching and mentoring, creating a culture of discipleship that perpetuates faith across generations.

### Mark 1:17

- **Context**: Jesus calls His first disciples early in His ministry, inviting them to follow Him and become "fishers of men."
- **What it teaches**: This statement emphasizes the active and relational nature of discipleship, where following Jesus involves reaching out to others.
- **Relevance**: It illustrates that discipleship is not merely a personal journey but one that actively engages others in the faith.
- **Application**: Believers are encouraged to share their faith actively and look for opportunities to engage with those who do not yet know Christ.

### Galatians 2:20

- **Context**: In this poignant declaration, Paul writes about the transformative experience of being a follower of Christ, reflecting on his own faith journey.
- **What it teaches**: Paul states, "I have been crucified with Christ and I no longer live, but Christ lives in me." This encapsulates the essence of being a disciple—allowing Christ to be at the center of one’s life.
- **Relevance**: It highlights the profound change that occurs when one becomes a disciple, emphasizing a life fully surrendered to Christ.
- **Application**: Christians are urged to reflect on how their lives can more fully reflect Christ’s presence, encouraging a deeper commitment to living out their faith.

### Colossians 3:16

- **Context**: Paul addresses the Colossian church, emphasizing the importance of community and the role of Scripture in the life of believers.
- **What it teaches**: This verse encourages believers to let the word of Christ dwell richly among them, teaching and admonishing one another through hymns and spiritual songs.
- **Relevance**: It reinforces the communal dimension of discipleship, where mutual encouragement and teaching among believers is vital.
- **Application**: Christians should cultivate an environment of teaching and mutual growth, sharing insights from Scripture and supporting one another in faith journeys.

## Understanding the Biblical Perspective

The passages discussed illustrate that discipleship is a multifaceted journey involving personal commitment, community engagement, and a continuous process of learning and teaching. At its core, discipleship is about following Jesus, embodying His teachings, and actively participating in the mission of the church. Common misconceptions about discipleship often include the idea that it is solely an individual pursuit or that it guarantees a life free from challenges. However, Scripture clarifies that discipleship is inherently relational, involving both a personal relationship with Christ and a commitment to others.

The biblical perspective on discipleship calls believers to recognize the transformative power of living in accordance with God's Word, to embrace the costs that come with following Jesus, and to actively engage in the process of teaching and mentoring others. By understanding these principles, believers can appreciate the rich tapestry of relationships and responsibilities that come with being a disciple of Christ.

## Practical Application

To apply the teachings of discipleship in daily life, believers can take several practical steps. First, individuals should prioritize time in Scripture, allowing God’s Word to shape their thoughts and actions. This can be done through personal study, group Bible studies, or participating in church teaching. Second, Christians are encouraged to connect with their church community, engaging in fellowship and mutual support. This could involve joining small groups, volunteering, or serving in ministries.

Additionally, believers should seek opportunities to mentor others, sharing their experiences and insights to guide new believers in their faith. This could be as informal as having coffee with a friend or as structured as leading a discipleship program. Lastly, reflecting on one’s daily choices and priorities in light of Jesus’ teachings can lead to a deeper commitment to living out the principles of discipleship.
